The Dichotomy of Interview Styles in the US and UK

January 23, 2015January 23, 2015Daniel Wen

Sitting at my first US university interview, I marvelled at the great disparity in the interview styles of American schools versus their British counterparts. This gulf between education systems is as deep and wide as the ocean that separates the two nations. Had I not felt nervous, I would have found the contrast comical.

Retrospectively, I should have been more astonished by my UK interviews. However, since I experienced them first, I had no preconceptions on what constitutes an interview. To their credit, Imperial College London conducted a session with me that qualified as an interview. Although the discussion was heavily academic, I enjoyed thinking out loud about how I would go about solving interesting scientific problems.

My interview with Cambridge was another matter; I would not call it an interview, but rather an interrogation.

“Interrogation” is not original. Others have heard it and relayed it to my ears. Admittedly, the word is slightly harsh, and a more precise description could be “a sample of the tutoring system.” In fact, my “interview” consisted of doing math and physics problems on a sheet of paper while my “interviewer” watched and, every so often, interjected. Little was spoken; I could count full sentences on my fingers. No words were expended on what I liked about the school, why I wanted to study engineering, or anything personal. With these memories fresh in my mind, I was amused by my US interview because it fell on the complete opposite end of the spectrum. I talked exclusively about my extracurricular activities, many of which have no pertinence whatsoever to my area of study. As a parallel example, one surely must play football in order to become an astrophysicist. Of course, diversity in the community is important, but US universities almost valorize this facet to the point of eclipsing the primary objective of educational institutions: to educate. Indeed, institutions of a certain reputation treat academic performance as secondary and regard it with enough narcissism. Based on what my interviewer said, they assume that, since you are applying to their glorious school, you must have the sufficient grades to do so. Therefore, there is no need to talk about academics.

US and UK universities have entirely different approaches to interviews. If they are among your options, whichever you choose depends on your outlook on school and life in general. I am tired of both.
